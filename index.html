Masters of the air.

Four species, four philosophies of flight. Each one perfected over millions of years of evolution.

Golden Eagle

A wingspan over two metres and eyesight eight times sharper than ours. The undisputed sovereign of mountain skies.

Hummingbird

Wings beating up to 80 times a second, hovering with impossible stillness. The only bird that can fly backwards.

Great Grey Owl

Silent flight and hearing so precise it can strike prey hidden beneath 30 centimetres of snow. A ghost of the northern forest.

Peregrine Falcon

The fastest animal alive. In a hunting dive it exceeds 380 km/h — faster than a Formula 1 car at full throttle.

Field Notes

A closer look at four lives on the wing.

Beyond the headline feats, each of these species tells a longer story — of where it lives, how it hunts, and the quiet adaptations that make its flight possible.

Golden Eagle

Aquila chrysaetos

A bird of open, mountainous country across the Northern Hemisphere, from the Scottish Highlands to the American Rockies. Mated pairs defend territories that can span a hundred square kilometres, riding thermals for hours as they scan the slopes below for hares and marmots. Their cliffside eyries are built of branches and reused — and enlarged — for generations.

Hummingbird

family Trochilidae

Found only in the Americas, from the forests of Alaska to the tip of Tierra del Fuego, and nowhere more diverse than the tropical Andes. A heart that races past 1,200 beats a minute demands enormous fuel, so they feed on nectar and small insects almost constantly — then, on cold nights, drop into a deep torpor that slows the body to a near standstill until dawn.

Great Grey Owl

Strix nebulosa

A phantom of the boreal forest, ranging across the far north of both North America and Eurasia. Its broad facial disc works like a radar dish, funnelling the faintest sound to ears set at slightly different heights — a trick that lets it pinpoint voles moving beneath the snow and plunge, talons first, through the crust to reach them.

Peregrine Falcon

Falco peregrinus

The most widespread raptor on Earth, at home on every continent but Antarctica — and increasingly on city skyscrapers, which stand in for the sea cliffs it once favoured. In its hunting stoop it folds into a teardrop and dives; bony baffles in its nostrils tame the rush of air, letting it breathe at speeds that would collapse another bird's lungs.

Did You Know?

The Arctic Tern flies 70,000 km every year.

From pole to pole and back again — the longest migration on Earth. Over a lifetime, that's three round-trips to the Moon.
